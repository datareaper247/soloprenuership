# Exit Engineering Engine — Build to Sell from Day 1

## Auto-Trigger (No Slash Command Needed)

Fires automatically when:
- "thinking about selling" / "exit" / "acquisition" → full exit engineering protocol
- "what's my company worth" → multi-method valuation (use `soloos valuation` CLI + this)
- "someone reached out to acquire us" → inbound acquisition response protocol
- "want to raise a Series A" → strategic vs financial acquirer mapping first
- "want to take chips off the table" → secondary sale + recapitalization options
- "building for 5 years then exit" → backwards exit engineering from day 1

---

## Why This Exists

Most founders think about exit 2-3 years too late.

The founder who builds for acquisition from month 1:
- Chooses ICP that creates strategic value for a known set of acquirers
- Avoids customer concentration (which creates acquisition risk)
- Documents every process (removing owner dependency = 30-40% multiple premium)
- Builds the acquirer relationships before being "for sale"

The founder who thinks about exit at the end:
- Is emotionally attached to a valuation that doesn't match market reality
- Has 90% of revenue in 3 customers (deal-killer for most acquirers)
- Has no documented processes (acquirer sees a job, not a business)
- Accepts the first term sheet without competitive tension

**Chanakya's Vijigishu principle**: The wise king positions himself as sought-after, not available. The moment you say "I want to sell," your leverage disappears. The goal is to make acquirers come to you.

## THE EXIT PATH SELECTION FRAMEWORK

### Which Exit to Optimize For — And Why It Changes Everything

```
EXIT PATH ANALYSIS (fire first before any exit discussion):
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

PATH 1: STRATEGIC ACQUISITION (highest valuation, hardest to achieve)
→ Who: Large companies that need what you've built
→ Why they pay premium: Speed to market, talent, customer base, IP
→ Typical multiple: 4-10x ARR (can be much higher with proprietary data/distribution)
→ ICP that creates strategic value: Enterprise, specific vertical, scarce expertise
→ Timeline: 2-5 years of relationship building before close
→ Optimize for: NRR (shows customers are sticky), growth rate, proprietary moat
→ Examples: Figma ($20B to Adobe), Mailchimp ($12B to Intuit), Zapier (profitable, strategic)

PATH 2: FINANCIAL ACQUISITION (fastest close, lower multiple)
→ Who: Private equity, search funds, independent sponsors, micro-PE
→ Why they pay: Predictable cash flow, multiple expansion potential, operational improvements
→ Typical multiple: 2.5-5x ARR (or 2.5-4x SDE for profitable businesses)
→ ICP that attracts: SMB or mid-market, clear processes, "boring" but essential category
→ Timeline: 3-6 months from engagement to close
→ Optimize for: SDE (Seller Discretionary Earnings), low owner dependency, clean financials
→ Target acquirers: Tiny Capital, Calm Company Fund, Relay Commerce, Permanent Equity,
                    Searchfunder deals, Flippa/Acquire.com for smaller deals

PATH 3: MARKETPLACE EXIT (fastest, lowest multiple, most accessible)
→ Who: Individual buyers, operators, micro-PE via marketplaces
→ Multiple: 1-4x ARR / revenue (depending on size and quality)
→ Minimum viable size: $5K+ MRR typically (lower for Flippa)
→ Best platforms by size:
  - <$50K ARR: Flippa (highest volume, lower quality buyers)
  - $50K-500K ARR: Acquire.com (formerly MicroAcquire) — fastest close
  - $500K-3M ARR: Empire Flippers, FE International (vetted buyers, higher multiples)
  - $3M+ ARR: Quiet Light Brokerage, QuietLight, or direct

PATH 4: PARTIAL EXIT (take chips off the table, keep equity)
→ Who: PE firms doing recapitalizations, growth equity
→ How: Sell 40-70% to PE, keep running the company, second bite at exit
→ Threshold: $1M+ ARR, profitable or near-profitable
→ Best for: Founders who want liquidity but aren't ready to stop building
→ Caveat: You now have a PE board. Different game.

WHICH PATH IS RIGHT FOR YOU?
Strategic → if you have unique IP, distribution moat, or proprietary data
Financial → if you have clean SDE and don't want an office job post-acquisition
Marketplace → if you want speed and simplicity over maximum valuation
Partial → if you want liquidity without losing the company
```

## THE ACQUIRER TARGETING SYSTEM

### Building the Relationship Before You're For Sale

```
STRATEGIC ACQUIRER IDENTIFICATION:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

STEP 1: Map the "Tuck-In Sweet Spot"
Who are the companies 5-20x your ARR that need what you do?
→ They can't build it fast enough
→ Their customers keep asking for it
→ A competitor has it and they don't

HOW TO FIND THEM:
→ Your top 5 integration partners: companies already in your customer's workflow
→ Companies that acquired 2+ companies in your category in the last 3 years
→ LinkedIn: search "[your category] partnerships", "[your category] acquisition"
→ Crunchbase: filter "acquiree" in your SIC code for recent acquisitions
→ Conference sponsors in your category: they're spending = they value the category

STEP 2: The Pre-LOI Relationship Strategy (18-month runway)
→ Month 1-3: Follow their product team on Twitter/X. Engage authentically.
→ Month 3-6: Publish content that addresses their strategic gaps. Tag them.
→ Month 6-9: Reach out for a "partnership" or "integration" conversation.
  DO NOT MENTION ACQUISITION. "I'd love to explore how we could build together"
→ Month 9-12: Deepen the integration. Get into their partner ecosystem.
→ Month 12-18: If interest is there, let them bring up acquisition.
  If not: the relationship still has value as a distribution partner.

"The best acquisition conversations start with partnership conversations."

STEP 3: The Soft Signal Audit
Signs an acquirer is interested (watch for these):
→ They ask about your customer data / market size
→ They ask "what are your long-term plans?"
→ They ask who else you're talking to
→ A BD person asks to meet your technical founder
→ They offer a "partnership" that sounds like it gives them too much

FINANCIAL ACQUIRER TARGET LIST (Micro-PE for bootstrapped SaaS):
→ Tiny Capital (tiny.com): Technical products, high GM, profitable. $500K-10M ARR.
→ Calm Company Fund (calmfund.com): Bootstrapped SaaS. Revenue share + equity deals.
→ Relay Commerce (relaycommerce.io): E-commerce SaaS specifically. $100K+ ARR.
→ Permanent Equity (permanentequity.com): Hold forever model. Profitable businesses.
→ Enduring Ventures (enduringventures.com): Small profitable SaaS. Values customer retention.
→ XO Capital: SMB SaaS. $1M-10M ARR.
→ Searchfunder community: Search funds for $500K-5M ARR profitable businesses.
```

## VALUATION OPTIMIZATION ROADMAP

### The 6 Levers That Move Your Multiple

```
MULTIPLE OPTIMIZATION (12-MONTH PRE-EXIT PLAN):
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

LEVER 1: NET REVENUE RETENTION (biggest single lever — ±2-3x multiple)
Current → Target:
<90% NRR: Must fix before any exit conversation. Acquirers will 50% haircut the price.
90-100% NRR: Acceptable. Work on expansion revenue.
100-110% NRR: Premium territory. Calls this out in every acquirer conversation.
>110% NRR: Elite. Will command top of range multiple.
HOW TO IMPROVE NRR:
→ Add a usage-based upsell tier
→ Add a seat expansion model if multi-user
→ Identify and remove churn triggers (exit interviews on every churned account)
→ Add customer success touchpoints at 30, 60, 90 days

LEVER 2: GROWTH RATE (second biggest lever)
Each 10% improvement in YoY growth rate = ~0.5-1x higher multiple
HOW TO IMPROVE:
→ Fix retention before improving growth rate
→ Identify highest-converting acquisition channel
→ 3 consecutive months of accelerating growth = "acceleration" story for acquirers

LEVER 3: GROSS MARGIN (>80% = +1x multiple)
SaaS target: >75% gross margin
Warning: AI API costs can compress this to 40-60%
HOW TO IMPROVE:
→ Audit every line of COGS: hosting, API calls, support headcount, payment processing
→ Renegotiate API costs at scale (Anthropic, OpenAI both have volume discounts)
→ Move low-margin support to in-app resources + AI

LEVER 4: OWNER DEPENDENCY (removing you = +30-40% to price)
Test: "If you disappeared for 6 months, what would happen?"
If the answer is "it would break" → this is the most urgent lever to fix.
HOW TO FIX:
→ Document every process (SOP for every recurring task)
→ Hire/automate at least 1 customer-facing function before exit
→ Build a customer success protocol that doesn't require founder judgment

LEVER 5: CUSTOMER CONCENTRATION (<20% revenue in single account)
Rule: No single customer should be >20% of MRR before exit
>30% concentration = red flag that can kill or delay deal
HOW TO FIX:
→ Actively grow smaller accounts
→ Do NOT renew large accounts at too-generous pricing (makes concentration worse)
→ Add at minimum 2 accounts to your "top 5" before exit process starts

LEVER 6: FINANCIAL DOCUMENTATION (accrual accounting = premium)
Many bootstrapped founders use cash-basis accounting. Acquirers want accrual.
3 months pre-exit minimum: switch to accrual + clean up books.
HOW TO PREPARE:
→ 3 years of clean P&L (or full company history if <3 years)
→ MRR/ARR split: new, expansion, contraction, churn — by month
→ CAC by channel (not just blended)
→ Cohort retention table (L30, L60, L90, L180, L365)
```

## THE 12-MONTH PRE-EXIT TIMELINE

```
MONTH 12-9 BEFORE EXIT:
□ Retain a fractional CFO/bookkeeper (clean up financial statements)
□ Switch to accrual-basis accounting if not already
□ Document all critical processes (SOP for every repeatable task)
□ Identify and address customer concentration issues
□ Begin building relationships with 3 target acquirers (partnership framing)

MONTH 9-6 BEFORE EXIT:
□ Reduce monthly churn to <3% if not already
□ Build expansion revenue mechanism (upsell tier, seat expansion)
□ Hire or delegate at least 1 customer-facing function away from founder
□ Build comparison page (yourproduct.com/vs/competitor-name) for SEO + acquirer awareness
□ Create CIM (Confidential Information Memorandum) — 15-page company overview

MONTH 6-3 BEFORE EXIT:
□ Formal NDA template prepared
□ Data room structure set up (Notion, Google Drive, or Docsend)
□ 3 years of financials audited or reviewed (not just self-prepared)
□ Customer reference list of 5-10 willing to speak with acquirer
□ IP audit: confirm all code, content, and brand assets owned by company entity

MONTH 3-0 BEFORE EXIT:
□ Begin formal process OR targeted direct approach to 3-5 strategic acquirers
□ If using a broker: engage 3 months out, negotiate commission (typical: 3-8% of deal)
□ Never approach a single buyer without at least 2 more in parallel (no leverage otherwise)
□ Prepare for due diligence: security audit, technical documentation, customer contracts
```

## INBOUND ACQUISITION RESPONSE PROTOCOL

### When Someone Reaches Out to Acquire You

```
INBOUND ACQUISITION PROTOCOL:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

THE CARDINAL RULE: Never appear eager. Appear interested but fully occupied.
Wrong: "We've been thinking about this too! When can we talk?"
Right: "We appreciate your interest. We're heads-down right now but could make time
        for an exploratory conversation in a couple weeks."

Day 1-3: Warm acknowledgment, express interest, DO NOT share financials.
  Email template: "Thanks for reaching out. [Company name] is doing well —
  we're always open to conversations about strategic fit.
  Can you share more about what's driving your interest?"

Day 3-7: "Exploratory call" to understand:
  → Their acquisition thesis (why you specifically?)
  → Their check size history (are they serious?)
  → Timeline (are they shopping or serious?)
  → Who is on their side (BD only or business unit + finance?)
  Red flags: No specific thesis, won't share prior deal sizes, only BD (not business unit)

Day 7-14: Send NDA + teaser deck ONLY
  Teaser deck: 5-8 slides. Revenue, growth rate, NRR, ICP, market size.
  NO: customer names, employee names, detailed tech, unit economics breakdown.
  NDA must include: no-hire clause for 24 months, non-solicit for customers

Day 14-30: Full CIM after NDA signed
  CIM structure:
  1. Executive summary (1 page)
  2. Business overview (2 pages)
  3. Product and tech overview (2 pages)
  4. Market opportunity (1 page)
  5. Financial summary (3 years) (2 pages)
  6. Growth strategy (1 page)
  7. Team and operations (1 page)
  8. Appendix: detailed financials, customer cohorts, tech architecture

Day 30+: Term sheet negotiation
  Leverage creation: "We have several parties interested. We're hoping to complete
  our process by [date]. Can you share your thinking on valuation range?"

HOW TO CREATE COMPETITIVE TENSION (without lying):
→ "We've had several inbound conversations recently" (true if you've talked to anyone)
→ "Our board has been having preliminary conversations" (advisory board counts)
→ Never lie about other offers — it's discoverable and kills trust permanently

WHEN TO HIRE AN M&A ADVISOR:
→ ARR >$500K: strongly consider a broker
→ ARR >$1M: broker or M&A advisor almost always worth the 5-8% fee
→ First-time exit: always hire someone (you've never done this before, they've done 100x)
→ Best brokers by size: Quiet Light ($500K-10M ARR), Empire Flippers (online businesses),
                          Corum Group (larger strategic deals)
```

## DEAL STRUCTURE INTELLIGENCE

### What to Watch in Term Sheets

```
TERM SHEET DECODER:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

STRUCTURE 1: ALL CASH AT CLOSE (best for seller)
→ Full payment on day of close
→ Best for: marketplace deals, financial acquirers
→ Risk: None (you get paid regardless of future performance)

STRUCTURE 2: CASH + EARNOUT (watch this carefully)
→ Portion paid at close, rest based on hitting future targets
→ Common: "30% paid at close, 70% over 3 years if hitting revenue targets"
→ Trap: Acquirer controls the product after close. They can kill growth to avoid paying earnout.
→ Acceptable earnout: Short (12-18 months max), measurable metrics, your control
→ Unacceptable: >24 months, vague metrics ("market penetration"), depends on acquirer behavior

STRUCTURE 3: EQUITY ROLLOVER (risky but can be lucrative)
→ You take part of purchase price in acquirer equity
→ Example: $5M deal = $3M cash + $2M in acquirer stock
→ Pros: Bigger upside if acquirer is growing
→ Cons: Liquidity risk, lock-up periods, no public market
→ Only accept if: Acquirer has clear IPO path or strong secondary market

RED FLAGS IN TERM SHEETS:
→ Broad indemnification clauses (can claw back your payment post-close)
→ Reps & warranties that cover everything (not just material misstatements)
→ Working capital adjustment without clear formula (can reduce price by $100K+ post-close)
→ Earnout on metrics you can't control
→ Non-compete >2 years in your category (industry standard is 18-24 months)
→ Key employee retention requirements you can't guarantee

THE WORKING CAPITAL ADJUSTMENT:
Most sophisticated acquirers include a working capital adjustment in the LOI.
"Final price = base price + (actual working capital at close) - (target working capital)"
If you don't have a baseline defined, the acquirer defines it in their favor.
Always negotiate the working capital target BEFORE signing the LOI.

REPS & WARRANTIES INSURANCE:
→ Available for deals >$2M
→ Insures the buyer against your representations being wrong
→ Cost: 2-4% of insured amount (paid by seller or split)
→ Benefit: Reduces your escrow requirement (from 10-15% to 3-5% of deal)
→ Worth getting for deals >$5M
```

## THE EXIT TIMING FRAMEWORK

### Sun Tzu's TIAN — Timing Is a Weapon

```
OPTIMAL TIMING ASSESSMENT:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

THE "SELL IN THE SPRING" PRINCIPLE:
→ Sell after a strong quarter, never after a bad one
→ Q1 results strong? Perfect time to initiate conversations
→ Q3 bad? Wait for Q4 recovery before starting any process

MARKET CONDITIONS CHECK:
→ Public SaaS multiples (use SaaS Capital or Meritech Index): rising = good time
→ M&A activity in your category: if competitors are being acquired, market is hot
→ Competitor funding rounds: a competitor raising Series B creates urgency for acquirers

PERSONAL TIMING:
→ Founder fatigue: selling while slightly tired is fine. Selling during burnout = bad decisions.
→ "Would I be happy building this for 2 more years?" If no → initiate now.
→ "Would I regret selling if the company was worth 3x more in 2 years?" If yes → wait.
→ Chanakya: "Begin a task only when the time is right and the preparations are complete."

THE "SECOND VALLEY" RISK:
Many companies hit a growth peak → plateau → second growth phase.
Sellers who wait for the second valley sometimes never find it.
If you're at peak growth: selling now captures the narrative before the plateau.
If you're in plateau: wait for re-acceleration OR sell on the next strong quarter.

ACQUISITION MARKET SIGNALS THAT CREATE URGENCY:
→ Competitor just got acquired: Category is validated. Strike while hot.
→ Major player entered your category: Threatens your moat. Exit before erosion.
→ VC backing of a competitor: 18-36 months until they dominate. Exit in 12.
→ Platform change (Apple iOS, Google algorithm): If dependent on a platform,
  exit before next platform shift creates uncertainty.
```

## THE DATA ROOM CHECKLIST

### 25 Documents for a Clean Exit Process

```
DATA ROOM CHECKLIST:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

FINANCIAL (prepare 12 months before exit):
□ P&L statements (3 years, accrual basis, monthly)
□ Balance sheet (current)
□ Cash flow statement (12 months)
□ MRR bridge by month (new, expansion, contraction, churn)
□ CAC by channel (12 months)
□ LTV by cohort
□ Gross margin breakdown (by cost category)
□ Cap table (if any investors or option grants)

LEGAL:
□ Certificate of incorporation + any amendments
□ Operating agreement or bylaws
□ All customer contracts (or template if SaaS/TOS-based)
□ IP assignment agreements (from all contractors and employees)
□ Non-disclosure agreements (outbound: what you've shared)
□ Any pending litigation or IP disputes
□ Employment agreements (if any employees)

TECHNICAL:
□ Architecture diagram (high-level, not full code)
□ Technology stack documentation
□ Third-party dependency list (APIs, libraries, SaaS tools)
□ Security audit or SOC 2 (if enterprise customers)
□ Disaster recovery and backup procedures
□ Infrastructure costs (AWS/GCP/Vercel — monthly breakdown)

CUSTOMERS:
□ Customer list (anonymized until NDA + advanced diligence)
□ Retention cohort table (L30, L60, L90, L180, L365)
□ Net Promoter Score (or Sean Ellis results)
□ Top customer concentration analysis (% revenue per top 10)
□ 5-10 customer references willing to speak with acquirer

OPERATIONS:
□ Org chart (even if it's just you)
□ Key employee/contractor agreements
□ Vendor agreements (critical subscriptions)
□ SOP documentation for key processes
□ Known issues, bugs, or technical debt (be honest — they'll find it anyway)
```
