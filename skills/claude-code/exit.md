## Why This Exists

Solo founders systematically undervalue their exits:
- **No banker**: Accept the first offer because they don't know there are others
- **No M&A framework**: Sign bad earnouts they don't understand
- **No pre-exit optimization**: Sell at 3x ARR when 6x was achievable with 6 months of prep
- **No acquirer map**: Wait for inbound when targeted outbound creates competition
- **No timing sense**: Sell in a down market or after growth decelerates

**The data that changes this**: Bootstrapped SaaS companies with >$1M ARR that run a structured process (competitive tension, pre-exit optimization, right timing) achieve 1.5-2.5x higher multiples than those that sell via inbound only.

**Chanakya's principle**: "The king who seeks alliance from a position of strength receives better terms than the king who seeks alliance from desperation." Never look like you need to sell.

## WHY EXIT ENGINEERING STARTS ON DAY 1

### The Exit Thesis Principle

Before writing a line of code or sending a cold DM, answer:

```
EXIT THESIS CHECK (fires when "building for exit" is mentioned):
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
Who is the most likely acquirer of this company in 5-7 years?
  → Name the category of buyer (strategic, PE, search fund)
  → Name 3 specific companies or firms that would want what you're building

Why would they pay a premium (vs. build it themselves)?
  → Speed (12-18 months to replicate your moat)
  → Customer relationships (your ICP is their ICP)
  → Data (proprietary dataset they cannot recreate)
  → Distribution (your channel is their gap)
  → Talent (your capability they lack)

What metric does your likely acquirer value most?
  → Strategic: ARR + NRR + customer brand logos
  → PE/Financial: EBITDA + growth rate + owner-independence
  → Search Fund: SDE + process documentation + defensibility

This answer shapes every product, pricing, and partnership decision from Day 1.
```

### 3 Exit Paths — Choose One, Optimize for It

```
EXIT PATH SELECTION:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

PATH 1: STRATEGIC ACQUISITION
Buyer: A larger company that needs your capability, distribution, or customers
Timeline: 5-10 years (usually)
Typical multiple: 5-15x ARR (premium for strategic value)
Who this fits: Enterprise ICP, integrations-heavy, category-defining
What to optimize: ARR growth, NRR, customer logos, switching cost depth
Acquirer examples: Salesforce, HubSpot, Adobe, Atlassian, ServiceNow
                   (buy companies that integrate with their platform or serve their ICP)

PATH 2: FINANCIAL ACQUISITION (PE / Search Fund / Micro-PE)
Buyer: A financial buyer who wants cash flow, not technology
Timeline: 3-7 years
Typical multiple: 2.5-5x SDE or 3-6x ARR
Who this fits: SMB ICP, high margins, low churn, defensible niche
What to optimize: SDE (owner's earnings), process documentation, churn reduction
Acquirer examples: Tiny Capital, Calm Company Fund, Chenmark, Permanent Equity,
                   FE International, Relay Commerce, Enduring Ventures, Ramp Capital
                   Search funds (Stanford, HBS, Wharton programs — 200+ active)

PATH 3: IPO / SPAC
Buyer: Public market
Timeline: 7-15 years
Threshold: $50M+ ARR with +30% YoY growth
Who this fits: Venture-backed, large TAM, winner-take-most categories
What to optimize: NRR, growth rate, gross margin, market share
Reality check for solo founders: Rare. If this is your thesis, you need VC co-pilots.

DEFAULT RULE: Choose Path 1 or Path 2. If Path 2, start optimizing EBITDA by $10K MRR.
If Path 1, delay profitability to maximize ARR growth — very different decisions.
```

### How Exit Thesis Affects Every Early Decision

| Decision | Path 1 (Strategic) | Path 2 (Financial) |
|---|---|---|
| ICP | Enterprise / Mid-Market | SMB — high volume, low touch |
| Pricing | Land-and-expand, usage-based | Simple subscription, annual plans |
| Gross Margin | 70%+ acceptable | 80%+ required |
| Hiring | Build team (strategic acquirers want teams) | Stay lean (PE wants SDE, not payroll) |
| Integrations | Deep integrations with likely acquirer's ecosystem | Standard integrations only |
| Revenue model | NRR > 110% = strategic premium | Low churn > expansion |
| Personal brand | Matters less (company sale) | Matters more (show transferability) |

### The Acqui-Hire Trap

```
⚠️ ACQUI-HIRE WARNING (fires when "team acquisition" or "talent deal" mentioned):
An acqui-hire pays $1-5M per engineer. Not a company valuation.
If you optimize for acqui-hire: you are optimizing for a job offer, not a business exit.
Acqui-hire signals: no product revenue, team is the asset, technology not commercialized.

Ask: "Would they want this without the team?"
If No → acqui-hire territory → recalibrate toward product/revenue exits instead.
```

## THE ACQUIRER TARGETING SYSTEM

### Strategic Acquirers

```
STRATEGIC ACQUIRER IDENTIFICATION PROTOCOL:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

STEP 1: The "Tuck-In Sweet Spot" Rule
Strategic acquirers typically buy companies that are:
→ 5-10% of their own ARR (large enough to matter, small enough to integrate)
→ Serving their existing ICP with an adjacent capability
→ Building what the acquirer has on their product roadmap
→ Already integrated with their platform (partner → acqui-hire path)

Example: If you build a scheduling tool for HubSpot users at $2M ARR,
and HubSpot is at $2B ARR — you are exactly in their tuck-in sweet spot.

STEP 2: Job Posting Intelligence
  → Search LinkedIn Jobs: "[capability you provide] engineer" at target companies
  → If they're hiring for what you do → they WANT this capability
  → If they've been hiring for 6+ months without filling → they NEED to acquire it
  → Job postings are your market research on acquirer intent

STEP 3: Integration Partner → Acquisition Pipeline
  → Build a native integration with the likely acquirer's platform
  → Get into their app marketplace
  → Appear in their "recommended tools" documentation
  → Attend their developer conferences
  → This is the pre-LOI relationship — they know you before you're for sale

STEP 4: Acquisition History Analysis
  → Search "[target company] acquires" on Crunchbase / TechCrunch
  → What size? What stage? What category? What multiple paid?
  → Pattern: acquirers repeat their acquisition thesis
  → If Salesforce acquires $2-10M ARR SaaS companies in your category → that's your data point

STEP 5: The "Already Told You" Method
  → Every time an enterprise prospect says "we use [company] for everything else"
  → That company is on your acquirer map
  → They want their ecosystem to be complete — your product fills a gap they see in sales
```

```
→ E-commerce (own brand): 2-3x SDE
→ Agency/services: 1.5-2.5x SDE (people-dependent = risk discount)

WHAT FINANCIAL ACQUIRERS OPTIMIZE FOR:
1. EBITDA / SDE (owner earnings): The primary valuation anchor
2. Growth rate: Even 10-20% growth YoY adds meaningful multiple premium
3. Owner dependency: Zero founder dependency = maximum multiple
   (can you take a 3-month vacation without revenue declining?)

5. Contract length: Annual contracts > monthly (predictability premium)
6. Churn: <2%/month is benchmark; >5%/month is a haircut
```

## VALUATION OPTIMIZATION ROADMAP

### The 6 Levers That Move Your Multiple

```
MULTIPLE OPTIMIZATION ENGINE:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

LEVER 1: NET REVENUE RETENTION (Biggest Multiple Driver)
Impact: From 2x ARR (NRR <90%) to 5x+ ARR (NRR >130%)
What it is: If customers who existed 12 months ago now pay more (expansion) or less (churn)
NRR = (Starting MRR + Expansion - Contraction - Churn) / Starting MRR × 100

NRR benchmarks and multiple impact:
<90%: -2x to -3x multiple (shrinking revenue = existential signal)
90-100%: Base multiple (flat — acceptable)
100-110%: +1x (customers staying flat to growing)
110-120%: +2x (strong expansion revenue — Slack, Datadog pattern)
>120%: +3x or more (net negative churn — gold standard)

How to move NRR in 6-12 months:
→ Add usage-based pricing tier that expands naturally with customer growth
→ Add seat-based pricing (each new hire = revenue)
→ Build in-app upgrade prompts at natural expansion moments
→ Proactive CS outreach at 60% usage capacity ("you're about to need the next tier")

LEVER 2: GROWTH RATE (Second Biggest Driver)
Impact: 2x ARR (flat) to 12x ARR (100%+ YoY)
Acquire.com data (2024): Bootstrapped SaaS sells at:
  Flat/declining: 1.5-2.5x ARR
  10-20% YoY growth: 2.5-4x ARR
  20-50% YoY growth: 4-6x ARR
  50%+ YoY growth: 6-10x ARR

Action: Record growth rate quarterly. Sell after 2-3 quarters of acceleration.
"Sell in the spring" principle: Sell after a strong quarter, never after a bad one.

LEVER 3: GROSS MARGIN (>80% Adds 1x)
SaaS benchmark: 75-85% gross margin
Below 60%: Risk discount (-1x to -2x)
Above 80%: Premium (+0.5x to +1x)
Above 85% + NRR >110%: Exceptional premium

Common gross margin killers in indie SaaS:
→ AI/LLM API costs at scale (OpenAI, Anthropic, Replicate)
→ Unmonitored infrastructure costs scaling with users
→ Excessive human support for "automated" product
→ Free tier users consuming significant infrastructure

Fix: Model gross margin at 10x current user count before exit.

LEVER 4: CUSTOMER CONCENTRATION RISK
Rule: No single customer >15-20% of revenue
>20% in one account: -0.5x to -1x multiple (risk discount)
>30% in one account: Some buyers won't proceed at all

12-month fix: Add 5+ new customers at similar deal size; negotiate expansion with others.
Or: Disclose clearly and price the risk into your expectations.

LEVER 5: OWNER DEPENDENCY (30-40% Haircut If Required)
Question acquirers ask: "What happens if this person takes 3 months off?"
→ If revenue stays flat: minimal dependency, maximum multiple
→ If revenue declines: dependency risk → discount applied

How to reduce before exit:
→ Document every recurring task as an SOP (hire a VA to follow the SOP before exit)
→ Move from founder-led sales to documented sales process (even 5 recorded calls)
→ Automate onboarding so new customers don't need founder involvement
→ Transition top 3 accounts from founder relationship to product relationship

LEVER 6: DOCUMENTED PROCESSES (SOPs for Everything = Premium)
Why it matters: Acquirer's question is "Can I run this without you?"
SOPs that matter most:
→ Customer acquisition SOP (how to get a new customer, step by step)
→ Onboarding SOP (what happens in the first 30 days)
→ Support SOP (how issues are resolved — including Tier 2 escalation)
→ Financial close SOP (monthly reconciliation, metric tracking)
→ Tech deployment SOP (how releases work, who has access)

Document BEFORE you think you need them. Buyers notice the gap.
```

### 12-Month Pre-Exit Optimization Checklist

```
PRE-EXIT TIMELINE:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

MONTH 12-9 (Financial Foundation):
□ Switch to accrual accounting (not cash basis) — buyers require this
□ Separate business and personal finances completely
□ Clean P&L with standard categories (no "misc" lines over $500)
□ Document MRR, ARR, churn, NRR, CAC, LTV in a metrics dashboard
□ Categorize all customers with start date, plan, MRR, last active date
□ Identify and begin addressing customer concentration (>20% single account)
□ Get 3 years of financials reviewed by a CPA (not necessarily audited, but reviewed)

MONTH 9-6 (Business Quality):
□ Document all recurring processes as SOPs (start with the top 10)
□ Hire a VA or contractor to run at least 3 recurring processes using your SOPs
□ Reduce churn: identify top 5 churn reasons, fix at least 2
□ Increase NRR: add one expansion mechanism (usage tier, seat-based, add-on)
□ Ensure all IP is assigned to the company entity, not the founder personally
□ Review all contracts — any handshake deals that aren't documented?
□ Tech documentation: architecture diagram, tech stack, all external API dependencies

MONTH 6-3 (Acquirer Relationship Building):
□ Identify 10-15 target acquirers using the Acquirer Targeting System
□ Begin genuine relationship building — NO mention of exit
  → Engage with their content, attend their events, request partnership calls
  → The goal: become a known name before you're for sale
□ Register at Acquire.com / Empire Flippers to understand market pricing
□ Begin drafting your CIM (Confidential Information Memorandum)
□ Retain an M&A advisor if ARR >$500K (broker fee pays for itself in higher multiple)
□ Increase pricing if below market — last chance before exit snapshot

MONTH 3-0 (The Process):
□ Finalize CIM and data room (25-document checklist below)
□ Set walk-away price (minimum acceptable) and target price
□ Decide: broker process or direct outreach or marketplace listing
□ Create "teaser" (1-page anonymous summary for first contact)
□ Brief any key employees who will need to know (handle carefully — timing is critical)
□ Execute the process: outreach → NDA → teaser → CIM → LOI → diligence → close
```

## INBOUND ACQUISITION RESPONSE PROTOCOL

Fires when someone reaches out with acquisition interest.

```
⚠️ INBOUND ACQUISITION RECEIVED — DO NOT SHARE FINANCIALS YET

FIRST RESPONSE TEMPLATE (Day 1-3):
"Thanks for reaching out — we've been thoughtful about our long-term direction,
and it's good to know [Company] sees value in what we're building.
Happy to explore if there's a fit. Can you share a bit more about your acquisition
thesis — what draws you to companies in this space right now? And what's your typical
deal structure?"

Goal of first response:
→ Warm but not eager (you have options — imply this)
→ Gather intelligence before revealing yours (who are they, why now, what size deals do they do)
→ Qualify: Is this serious or fishing? Have they done deals before? Can they write a check?

DAY 3-7: THE EXPLORATORY CALL
Questions to answer on this call:
1. "What's driving this interest at this particular moment?"
2. "Have you done similar acquisitions before — what did those look like?"
3. "What size deals does your team typically pursue?"
4. "If we found alignment, what would next steps look like on your side?"
5. "What's your timeline for decisions like this?"

DO NOT answer on this call:
→ Your exact ARR / revenue
→ Your profit margin
→ Your growth rate
→ Your customer count
→ Whether you "need" to sell

QUALIFYING SIGNALS (decide whether to continue):
Green: Has done acquisitions in your range before / has clear strategic rationale /
       knows your product before reaching out / timeline is specific
Yellow: First acquisition ever / "exploring the space" / vague rationale / no timeline
Red: Immediately wants financials before NDA / vague on deal size / no track record

DAY 7-14: THE TEASER (Send only after NDA signed)
1-page anonymous summary containing:
→ Business category and model (not name)
→ Revenue range (e.g., "$2-3M ARR")
→ Growth rate (e.g., "+35% YoY")
→ Customer segment (not names)
→ Why you're exploring a transaction (optional)

DAY 14-30: THE CIM (Full document, post-NDA, for serious parties only)
→ Confidential Information Memorandum (see template below)
→ Organized data room access (see checklist below)
→ Track: Who accessed what and when (signals seriousness level)

DAY 30+: TERM SHEET NEGOTIATION
→ Do not negotiate with a single party if you can help it
→ Creating competitive tension (even with 2 parties) is the single highest-leverage move
```

### Creating Competitive Tension

```
COMPETITIVE TENSION PLAYBOOK:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

The most powerful phrase in M&A: "We're in conversations with a few parties."

How to create legitimate competitive tension:
→ Reach out to 5-10 potential acquirers simultaneously (or use a broker to do this)
→ Set a "first round deadline" for LOIs: "We're collecting first-round indications by [date]"
→ List on a marketplace while running a direct process (two tracks simultaneously)
→ Let parties know the process has multiple participants (true, not a bluff)

What NOT to do:
→ Fabricate parties (it always surfaces and kills trust)
→ Reveal one party's terms to another (breach of NDA, legal exposure)
→ Use fake urgency that isn't real (sophisticated buyers see through it)

What TO do:
→ Run a structured process with a deadline — even if 2 parties, that is competition
→ If one party sends LOI: notify other parties "we've received an indication of interest
  and are moving toward exclusivity — if you'd like to submit, this week would be the time"
→ This is truthful and creates urgency without fabrication

The broker advantage at $500K+ ARR:
A good broker (FE International, Quiet Light, Empire Flippers) runs competitive process by default.
Their fee (10-15%) often pays for itself in the higher multiple from competitive tension.
At $1M ARR: 0.5x multiple improvement = $500K. Broker fee: $50-150K. Net positive.
```

### When to Hire an M&A Advisor

```
BROKER/ADVISOR DECISION FRAMEWORK:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

<$200K ARR: Self-serve (Acquire.com, Flippa)
  → Broker fee eats too much of deal value
  → Market is efficient at this size — listed price is market price

$200K-$500K ARR: Consider a broker for complex situations
  → Empire Flippers or Quiet Light if you want managed process
  → Self-serve is still viable with good documentation

$500K-$2M ARR: Strong recommendation to use a broker
  → Multiple gap from competitive tension typically exceeds fee
  → Broker validates financials → buyer discount decreases
  → You focus on running the business while process runs in parallel

$2M+ ARR: Almost certainly use an advisor
  → Structures get complex (earnouts, rollover equity, reps & warranties)
  → Tax optimization alone justifies advisor cost
  → Negotiating legal terms without experience here is significant risk

M&A attorney: Required at any deal size above $100K.
Do not sign an LOI without a lawyer reviewing it. Ever.
```

## DEAL STRUCTURE INTELLIGENCE

```
DEAL STRUCTURE DECODER:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

STRUCTURE TYPE 1: ALL-CASH AT CLOSE (Best for seller)
→ You receive 100% of deal value at closing
→ No performance risk post-sale
→ Buyer takes all integration and future performance risk
→ When you get this: strong financials, competitive process, clean business
→ When you don't: buyer uncertainty about future performance

STRUCTURE TYPE 2: EARNOUT (Most Common, Often a Trap)
What it is: A portion of the purchase price contingent on hitting post-close targets
→ "We'll pay $3M today + up to $1M more if you hit $2M ARR in 24 months"

When earnouts are acceptable:
→ You control the outcome (you stay on and run the business)
→ Targets are based on metrics you can influence
→ Earnout period is <24 months
→ Targets are no higher than your current trajectory would produce naturally

When earnouts are traps (decline or renegotiate):
→ You don't control the outcome post-acquisition (they integrate into their business)
→ Targets require performance beyond historical trajectory
→ Earnout period >24 months (too much uncertainty)
→ Targets are set on metrics that the acquirer's decisions will influence
→ "We'll hit targets together" — acquirers often change direction post-close

Chanakya principle: Never enter a contract where your counterparty controls
your ability to perform. Earnout tied to buyer's product decisions = wealth transfer trap.

STRUCTURE TYPE 3: SELLER FINANCING (Common in smaller deals)
What it is: You loan the buyer part of the purchase price, paid back over time
→ "We'll pay $500K cash + $200K over 3 years at 8% interest"
→ Common in search fund deals and smaller transactions

When to accept: When it closes a gap and buyer is creditworthy
When to avoid: When business is your primary wealth and you need liquidity
Interest rate standard: 6-10% (match or exceed bank rate)

STRUCTURE TYPE 4: ROLLOVER EQUITY (PE Deals Primarily)
What it is: You reinvest 10-30% of your proceeds back into the new entity
→ "We'll pay $4M + you roll $1M equity into the NewCo"

When it's valuable: PE firms with strong track record, clear exit strategy, legitimate growth plan
The appeal: If PE doubles or triples value in 5 years, your rollover pays 2-3x
The risk: Illiquidity + dependency on PE execution + minority position with limited control
Rule: Never roll equity into a PE deal without understanding their exit thesis for the company.

STRUCTURE TYPE 5: SECONDARY SALE / RECAPITALIZATION
What it is: You sell 50-70% of the company, maintain minority ownership, stay on as operator
→ Liquidity event without full exit
→ Access to PE's resources + capital while retaining upside

When to consider: $500K+ SDE, want to "take chips off the table," want growth capital
Who does this: Micro-PE, growth-oriented PE, family offices
The result: Partial liquidity now + substantial upside in second exit event
```

### Red Flags in Term Sheets

```
TERM SHEET RED FLAG SCANNER:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

🚩 EARNOUT >30% OF PURCHASE PRICE
  Meaning: They don't believe in the business at the stated price.
  Response: "Our preference is an all-cash structure. What would that look like?"

🚩 WORKING CAPITAL ADJUSTMENT WITH AMBIGUOUS DEFINITION
  Meaning: Most common source of post-close disputes.
  Response: Get your accountant to define a "working capital target" precisely
            before signing. Ambiguity here costs tens to hundreds of thousands.

🚩 "KEY EMPLOYEE" CLAUSES TIED TO YOUR EMPLOYMENT STAY
  Meaning: Your payout partially depends on you staying post-acquisition.
  Response: Negotiate the retention window down (12 months max vs. 36 months)
            and ensure it vests monthly, not cliff.

🚩 BROAD REPS & WARRANTIES WITHOUT INSURANCE
  Meaning: You can be sued for things you didn't know about post-close.
  Response: Ask for Reps & Warranties Insurance (R&W Insurance) for deals >$3M.
            The premium is typically 2-4% of the insurance limit; typically buyer pays.

🚩 EXCLUSIVITY LONGER THAN 60 DAYS
  Meaning: 30-45 days is standard; longer exclusivity is usually the buyer's leverage.
  Response: Negotiate down. If diligence isn't done in 60 days, extend by written agreement.

🚩 MATERIAL ADVERSE CHANGE (MAC) CLAUSE WITH BROAD DEFINITION
  Meaning: Buyer can walk away from the deal if "material adverse change" occurs.
  Response: Narrow the MAC definition specifically. "Material" should be >X% revenue change.

🚩 INDEMNIFICATION CAP BELOW 20% OF PURCHASE PRICE
  Meaning: If you made misrepresentations, buyer is under-protected → will be contentious.
  Response: Standard indemnification cap: 15-30% of purchase price, reasonable.
            Below 15% can mean buyer will try to recoup via lawsuit if they find issues.
```

## THE EXIT TIMING FRAMEWORK

```
OPTIMAL SALE TIMING ANALYSIS:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

THE "SELL IN THE SPRING" PRINCIPLE (Chanakya — TIAN timing):
Never sell after a bad quarter. Always sell at or near a peak.
Buyers anchor on trailing 12 months. Sell after 2-3 of your best quarters.

PUBLIC MARKET INDICATOR:
SaaS public market multiples are the floor for private market multiples.
→ When SaaS ETF (WCLD) or BVP NASDAQ Emerging Cloud Index is high → private multiples high
→ When public SaaS multiples contract → private buyers demand discounts too
→ Check: SaaS Capital Index, Bessemer Index, or public comps in your category

BUSINESS LIFECYCLE TIMING:
Optimal window: After a period of acceleration, BEFORE the next trough
Typical pattern: growth accelerates → plateau → investors discount the plateau
Sell during: acceleration phase (even if metrics will continue to improve — you can't know)
Avoid: Selling during stagnation, churn spike, or tech debt accumulation period

THE SECOND VALLEY RISK:
Many founders wait for "just one more good quarter"
→ The company hits a natural ceiling
→ Growth decelerates from 40% to 15% YoY
→ Buyer offers 30-40% less than the peak offer
→ The second offer never comes at the first offer's price again
Rule: If you receive an offer 70%+ of your target price and growth is showing early deceleration,
      evaluate seriously. The cost of waiting is highest at inflection points.

FOUNDER FATIGUE INDICATOR:
If you would take the deal at 80% of target price today = fatigue is affecting judgment
If you would only take it at 150% of target price = denial/anchoring bias
Honest test: "What would I do with the next 3 years if I didn't have this company?"
If the answer is more compelling than the company: sell sooner, sell well.

MARKET CONDITIONS TO TIME AROUND:
→ Low interest rates: PE firms have cheap debt → higher multiples → sell
→ High interest rates (2022-2024 environment): PE leverage costs more → lower multiples → wait or optimize
→ Strategic acquirer flush with cash (post-IPO, strong quarter): better window for strategic exit
→ Competitor just raised large round: creates strategic urgency for acquirers in your category
```

## CHANAKYA'S EXIT WISDOM

```
KOSHA — Optimize for Cash, Not Just Valuation:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
"A king who fills his treasury well shall never be weak."

A $5M deal with $4M at close beats a $6M deal with $3M at close + $3M earnout.

Kosha principle: Discount earnouts at 40-60% of face value when comparing structures.
$6M deal: $3M cash + $3M earnout at 50% discount = $4.5M effective value
$5M deal: $4M cash + $1M earnout at 50% = $4.5M effective value
Both equivalent — but the $5M deal has less risk. Prefer it.

VIJIGISHU — The Conqueror's Posture (Never Seem Available):
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
"The king who is sought out for alliance commands better terms
than the king who seeks alliance."

Vijigishu in practice:
→ Never proactively say you're "open to acquisition" to anyone who will talk to a buyer
→ Build the product publicly like you'll be building it for 10 more years
→ Accept inbound with warmth + optionality language ("interesting to explore")
→ When asked "would you ever sell?" → "We're focused on building — if the right partner
  appeared with the right vision for the product, we'd always listen"
→ This posture signals you don't need to sell → buyers compete to get your attention

NEVER SEEM DESPERATE:
→ Every email about "exciting acquisition opportunity" goes unanswered for 5-7 days
→ Every exploratory call should be scheduled 1-2 weeks out
→ Never volunteer your minimum price; anchor high and let them negotiate down
→ The more optionality you show (I have other options, I'm talking to others), the better terms

Sun Tzu parallel: "Appear weak when you are strong, and strong when you are weak."
In M&A: Appear indifferent when you want to sell.
```

```
→ Competitive positioning (why customers choose you; how you compare to alternatives)

→ NPS or customer satisfaction data (if available)
→ Testimonials and case studies (2-3)

SECTION 5: GROWTH STRATEGY (2-3 pages)
→ Current acquisition channels + CAC per channel
→ Untapped growth opportunities (framed as buyer upside)
→ Product roadmap (what's been built, what's planned — gives buyer a future picture)

SECTION 6: OPERATIONS (2-3 pages)
→ Team overview: current headcount, roles, FT vs contractor

→ Key vendor/partner relationships
→ Process documentation status (SOP maturity)

SECTION 7: THE DEAL (1-2 pages)
→ Transaction rationale: why selling now
→ Desired structure (cash, partial earnout, partial rollover — your preference)
→ Transition commitment: duration, scope
→ Timeline: when you'd like to close by
→ Process: how to proceed to next steps
```

### SDE/EBITDA Calculation Worksheet

```
SDE CALCULATION (for financial acquirers):
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

NET INCOME (from P&L):                    $[X]
Add back (legitimate add-backs):
  + Founder salary/draw:                  $[X]  (will not continue under new owner)
  + Owner health insurance:               $[X]
  + Owner retirement contributions:       $[X]
  + One-time legal/accounting fees:       $[X]  (non-recurring)
  + Non-cash depreciation:               $[X]
  + Interest expense (if debt):          $[X]
  + Personal expenses through business:  $[X]  (if any — must be justifiable)

Deduct (real costs that continue):
  - Market-rate salary for replacement GM: $[X]  (if you're doing ops work)
  - One-time revenue that won't recur:     $[X]

= SELLER'S DISCRETIONARY EARNINGS (SDE): $[X]

EBITDA VERSION (for larger deals/PE):
Same as SDE but WITHOUT adding back founder salary (assumes professional management).
EBITDA = Net Income + Interest + Taxes + Depreciation + Amortization (+ non-recurring items)

COMMON MISTAKES:
→ Adding back costs that are recurring (buyer's lawyer will claw this back)
→ Not adding back non-cash stock compensation (if applicable)
→ Missing legitimate add-backs (insurance, retirement, one-time legal)
```
