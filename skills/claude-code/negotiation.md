# NEGOTIATION — Tactical Negotiation Engine

Fires automatically when founder says:
- "they came back with a counteroffer"
- "how do I negotiate pricing" / "they want a discount"
- "contract negotiation" / "vendor is pushing back"

- "I don't know how to respond"

**Negotiation fires for ANY situation involving competing interests** — not just price. Hiring negotiation, vendor contracts, partnership terms, customer pricing, investor terms — all routes here.

---

## WHY THIS EXISTS

Chris Voss (Never Split the Difference, 2016) spent 24 years as an FBI hostage negotiator and found:

> "The most dangerous negotiating myth is that compromise is fair. Compromise means you both lose something. The goal is not to meet in the middle — it's to find a solution where both parties feel heard and their core interests are served."

Roger Fisher & William Ury (Getting to Yes, Harvard Negotiation Project) identified that most negotiation failures come from negotiating **positions** instead of **interests**. The stated position ("I want $50K/year") hides the real interest ("I need to cover my team's salaries and have budget left for testing").

**The solo founder's negotiation challenge**: You're negotiating without a team, without a procurement department, and often without a BATNA. This skill levels the field.

## THE VOSS TACTICAL EMPATHY FRAMEWORK

### Foundation: Before Any Negotiation Starts

```
PRE-NEGOTIATION INTELLIGENCE GATHERING:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Answer these 5 questions BEFORE your first move:

1. THEIR GOAL: What does the other party actually want?
   (Not what they say they want — what outcome would make them declare success?)

2. THEIR CONSTRAINTS: What can't they do? What are their real limits?
   (Budget cycle? Approval process? Competing priorities? Timeline?)

3. YOUR BATNA: What do you do if this deal falls through?
   (Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement — your real floor)
   Rule: If you don't know your BATNA, you can be pushed anywhere.

4. THEIR BATNA: What do they do if this deal falls through?
   (Understanding their alternative tells you how much leverage you have)
   If their BATNA is worse than yours: you have leverage you may not be using.

5. THE REAL INTERESTS: Separate positions from interests.
   Position = "We want a 20% discount"
   Interest = "We're over budget this quarter and our CFO needs to approve anything >$X"
   Solutions to interests are unlimited. Solutions to positions are one.
```

### The Core Voss Techniques (Apply Based on Situation)

#### Technique 1: Tactical Empathy + Labeling

**When to use**: Opening any negotiation, especially when emotions are elevated.

**How it works**: Name the other party's feelings and concerns before they need to say them. This disarms defensiveness and builds trust faster than any rational argument.

```
LABELING FORMULA: "It seems like..." / "It sounds like..." / "It looks like..."

NEVER: "I understand how you feel" (condescending, triggers resistance)
ALWAYS: "It seems like [their specific situation/feeling]"

EXAMPLES:
"It seems like budget timing is a real constraint here."
"It sounds like you've been burned by vendors who overpromised before."
"It looks like the approval process for this requires more than one person to say yes."

AFTER LABELING: Pause. Let them confirm or correct. Both outcomes are good.
- If they confirm: you've built rapport and named their real concern
- If they correct: "Actually it's [X]" — they've just told you what really matters

TRIPLE LABEL TECHNIQUE (for resistant parties):
Label their concern once. Then label again from a different angle. Then label a third time.
By the third label, most resistance collapses — people need to be heard, not convinced.
```

#### Technique 2: Calibrated Questions

**When to use**: When you need information, when you're stuck, when you want to move the other party without pressure.

**How it works**: "How" and "What" questions force the other party to problem-solve on your behalf.

```
CALIBRATED QUESTION BANK:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

"How am I supposed to do that?" (Voss's most powerful question — makes them solve your problem)
"What would need to be true for this to work for both of us?"
"How does this fit into your current situation?"
"What's the biggest challenge in making this decision?"
"How do you see this playing out from here?"
"What's most important to you about [this term / this deal / this timeline]?"
"What would make you feel good about this?"

WHAT TO AVOID:
"Why?" → Sounds accusatory, triggers defensive reasoning
"Can you...?" → Yes/No question, gives them easy out
"Would you consider...?" → You've just anchored low for yourself

THE CALIBRATED QUESTION PRINCIPLE:
Questions that start with "How" or "What" make the other party's brain work.
Their brain working on your behalf = you're in control without pressure.
```

#### Technique 3: The Accusation Audit

**When to use**: Before any ask that might generate resistance.

**How it works**: List all the negative things the other party might be thinking about you — then say them out loud first.

```
ACCUSATION AUDIT FORMULA:
"I know this might seem [negative thing], and I want to address that directly."
"You might be thinking [concern] — here's what I want you to understand..."

EXAMPLE (raising prices with existing customer):
"I know what you're thinking — we've raised prices and you're wondering whether the value
 justifies it. And I don't blame you for thinking that. Here's specifically what you're
 getting now that you weren't getting before: [specifics]."

WHY THIS WORKS: When you name the accusation first, the other party can't use it as a weapon.
                It's already on the table. Now you're both looking at it together.
```

#### Technique 4: The Anchor + Counter-Anchor

**When to use**: Price negotiations, contract term negotiations.

```
ANCHORING RULES:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

RULE 1: The person who anchors first controls the conversation.
        Let them anchor if you don't know their budget. Ask: "What budget do you have in mind?"
        Anchor first if you have good information about their willingness to pay.

RULE 2: Anchor extreme but reasonable.
        Too extreme = laughed out / relationship damage
        Reasonable = weakens your position before negotiation starts

RULE 3: When you receive a low anchor — never accept it or counter with 50%.
        Instead: use the "That's not going to work" response (below).

RULE 4: Precise numbers anchor better than round numbers.
        "$47,500" is more credible than "$50,000" — signals you've done the math.

THE COUNTER-ANCHOR SEQUENCE (after receiving an offer you won't accept):
1. "I appreciate the offer. That's not going to work for me."
   [Pause. Do not explain or justify yet.]
2. Wait for their response. If they ask why:
   "Based on [what we deliver], the value here is closer to [your anchor]."
3. If they counter: The midpoint is usually where you land.
   Move your anchor toward them in decreasing steps (e.g., $100K → $90K → $85K → $83K)
   The decreasing steps signal you're reaching your floor.

RULE 5: Never split the difference on the first counteroffer.
        "Let's split the difference at $X" means you had room the whole time.
        Splitting too early = you'll be asked to split again.
```

#### Technique 5: "That's Right" vs. "You're Right"

```
CRITICAL DISTINCTION:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

"You're right" = I want you to stop talking. Empty agreement. Conversation-ender.
               Often heard from someone who has checked out of the negotiation.

"That's right" = I feel genuinely understood. True agreement. Relationship deepener.
               The breakthrough moment in any negotiation. When you hear it, you've won.

HOW TO GET "THAT'S RIGHT":
Summarize their position so accurately that they feel completely understood.
"So what you're saying is: [their concern in their words, not yours]."
The moment they say "That's right, exactly" → they're ready to move toward yes.

IF YOU'RE SAYING "YOU'RE RIGHT" IN A NEGOTIATION:
You've stopped fighting for your position. Dangerous. Reanchor.
```

## THE FISHER/URY INTEREST-BASED FRAMEWORK

### Separate People From Problems

```
PEOPLE vs. PROBLEM SEPARATION:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

The relationship with the other party has value independent of this negotiation.
Treating their position as a personal attack = relationship damage + worse outcome.

TECHNIQUE: Distinguish between:
- The person: [Your customer Sarah] — who you want to maintain a relationship with
- The problem: [Pricing renewal disagreement] — which is what you're solving together

LANGUAGE PATTERN:
"I want us to find something that works for both of us here."
NOT: "I need you to understand our position."

"Help me understand your concern so we can address it."
NOT: "You need to understand that our price reflects our value."

The first set puts you on the same side of the table.
The second set puts you across from each other.
```

### Focus on Interests, Not Positions

```
INTEREST EXCAVATION TECHNIQUE:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

POSITION (what they ask for): "We need a 30% discount"
INTEREST (why they want it): Needs to hit budget. Scared to get approved by CFO.
                             Has been burned on ROI before. Wants to test before committing.

Each interest has multiple solutions. The position has one: lower your price.

HOW TO EXCAVATE INTERESTS:
Ask: "Help me understand what's driving the [position] — is it primarily [budget / timeline / authority / risk]?"
Or: "What's the most important factor for you in making this decision?"
Or: "If price weren't a constraint, what would you change about this deal?"

The answer to the last question tells you the REAL interest.
(If they say "nothing" — it actually is just price)
(If they say "X" — now you know what to trade price for)

INTERESTS TO ALWAYS CHECK:
□ Economic: Cost, ROI, budget cycle constraints
□ Procedural: Approval process, legal review, procurement rules
□ Psychological: Risk tolerance, relationship dynamics, need to look good to boss
□ Strategic: Timing, competitive considerations, roadmap fit
```

## SCENARIO PLAYBOOKS

### Scenario 1: Customer Wants a Discount (Pricing Negotiation)

```
CUSTOMER DISCOUNT PLAYBOOK:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

OPENING: Never discount immediately. Discounting without understanding why = you had margin.

STEP 1: Label the concern before responding.
"It sounds like budget is the main constraint here — is that right?"
(If yes: budget problem. If no: they just want a better deal. Different responses.)

STEP 2: If budget constraint is real — reframe to value, not price.
"Let me understand your situation better. What's the impact if [problem we solve] continues
for another [quarter / year]? What does that cost you?"
(Forces them to calculate the cost of NOT buying = makes price look small)

STEP 3: If they persist — offer an alternative structure, not a discount.
OPTIONS (each preserves your revenue while solving their constraint):
  □ Annual commitment: "I can't do 30% off monthly, but annual upfront = [20% off]"
  □ Phased implementation: "We start with [smaller scope] at [lower price] and expand in Q2"
  □ Delayed start: "We begin billing [X weeks] after implementation — buys you a quarter"
  □ Trade: "I can reduce price if you can [provide case study / 3-year commitment / referrals]"

STEP 4: If still stuck — the "How am I supposed to do that?" close.
"I hear you. I want to make this work. How am I supposed to get to [their number]?"
[Pause. Let them solve your problem.]

STEP 5: Know your floor and defend it.
Decide before the call: what is the minimum deal you'll accept?
Below that floor: walk away gracefully. "I think we're not at the right place yet.
Let's reconnect in [X months] when the timing might work better."
Leaving without desperation = you have a BATNA. Shows you have alternatives.
```

### Scenario 2: Vendor / Supplier Negotiation

```
VENDOR NEGOTIATION PLAYBOOK:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

LEVERAGE LEVERS (use in order):
1. Competitive alternatives: "We're evaluating [X, Y, Z] as well" — even if true only barely
2. Volume commitment: "If pricing works, we'd commit to [volume / annual]"
3. Payment terms: "If you can do [price], we can pay net-15 instead of net-60"
4. Case study / referral: "Could reduce the price in exchange for a case study"
5. Scope reduction: "If that's the price, what can we remove to hit our number?"

NEVER: Start with your bottom-line budget. Anchor high and concede down.

THE SILENCE TECHNIQUE:
After your counter-offer: stop talking. The first person to speak after the anchor loses.
Silence is pressure — let it work. Most founders fill silence with unnecessary concessions.
Count to 10 internally before speaking again.
```

### Scenario 3: Partnership / Deal Terms Negotiation

```
PARTNERSHIP DEAL PLAYBOOK:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

TERM SHEETS AND LETTERS OF INTENT:
Every term is negotiable. But never negotiate multiple terms simultaneously.

Anchor the most important term first. Agree on it.
Then move to the next term. Never reopen agreed terms.

CRITICAL: Understand which terms are truly non-negotiable (theirs and yours).
"Is this term negotiable, or is this a hard constraint for your organization?"
This question saves hours. If it's a hard constraint, stop spending capital there.

REVENUE SHARE / EQUITY NEGOTIATIONS:
1. The first number in an equity/revenue split is an anchor, not a starting point.
2. "What does that number represent to you?" — excavate the interest behind the share requested.
3. Often: someone asks for 20% equity because they want to feel like a "real partner."
   Alternative that serves the same interest: "preferred partner" status, board observer, advisory equity.

EXCLUSIVE VS. NON-EXCLUSIVE:
Exclusivity has a price. Always price it.
"I'm happy to discuss exclusivity — what's the additional value exchange for that restriction?"
Exclusivity without compensation = bad deal. Exclusivity with compensation = depends on the number.
```

### Scenario 4: Investor Term Negotiation

```
INVESTOR TERM PLAYBOOK:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

MOST IMPORTANT TERMS (rank them before any negotiation):
1. Valuation (dilution impact)
2. Pro-rata rights (future round control)
3. Board composition (governance control)
4. Liquidation preferences (exit economics)
5. Anti-dilution (down-round protection)

VOSS PRINCIPLE FOR INVESTORS: Never negotiate against yourself.
When an investor gives you terms you want to push back on:
"Help me understand the reasoning behind [specific term]."
Let them explain before you counter. Their explanation may reveal flexibility.

BATNA IN INVESTOR NEGOTIATIONS:
Your BATNA is: alternative investors / bootstrapping / current investors.
The stronger your BATNA, the stronger your negotiating position.
"We're in conversations with a few other investors" — only say if true, but say it.

COMMON INVESTOR TERMS TO NEGOTIATE:
□ Valuation: Negotiate with BATNA and comparable comps, not emotion
□ Pro-rata: "We want to preserve this for investors who add value beyond capital"
□ Information rights: "Monthly update is standard — quarterly for smaller checks"
□ Lead investor rights: "Lead investor gets enhanced rights — your check size determines this tier"

ALWAYS INCLUDE:
"This is not legal or financial advice. Engage qualified counsel before signing any term sheet."
```

## NEGOTIATION RESPONSE SCRIPTS

```
LIBRARY OF HIGH-LEVERAGE RESPONSES:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

WHEN THEY MAKE A LOW OFFER:
"I appreciate you putting a number on the table. That's not going to work for us.
 [Pause. Wait for them to respond or ask why.]"
→ Forces them to either justify or soften. Never fill this silence.

WHEN THEY PUSH BACK ON YOUR PRICE:
"I hear you. How am I supposed to get there when [specific value/cost driver]?"
→ Your problem is now their problem to solve.

WHEN YOU NEED TIME TO THINK:
"I need a moment to think about that — can we have [X hours/a day] before I respond?"
→ Always acceptable. Never make major concessions under time pressure.

WHEN YOU DON'T KNOW HOW TO RESPOND:
"Let me make sure I understand what you're proposing — [summarize their offer back accurately]."
→ Buys time AND signals careful listening. "That's right" response = you can proceed.

WHEN THEY SAY "FINAL OFFER":
"I understand that's where you are. I'm not sure we're going to be able to make this work
 at that level. What would I need to demonstrate to you to make [your number] possible?"
→ Tests whether "final offer" is real. Often it's not.

WHEN A DEAL IS BREAKING DOWN:
"Before we leave this conversation — is there anything that would make this work for both of us?"
→ The final attempt. Creates one last opening without desperation.
```

## THE CONCESSION LADDER

```
CONCESSION STRATEGY (use this structure for any negotiation):
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

BEFORE THE NEGOTIATION: Define your levels
Level 1 (ideal): What you want if they accept everything
Level 2 (acceptable): What works well for you
Level 3 (floor): Minimum you'll accept before walking away
Below floor: Walk away

DURING THE NEGOTIATION: Concede in DECREASING increments
First concession: Large step (shows willingness to move)
Second concession: Smaller step (signals you're working toward your limit)
Third concession: Very small step (signals you're near your floor)
Fourth+ concession: Tiny or conditional (you're at your limit)

EXAMPLE (price negotiation starting at $100K, floor at $75K):
Opening anchor: $100K
First concession: $90K (if necessary)
Second concession: $83K (smaller step)
Third concession: $78K (even smaller)
Final position: $76K (trivially small step = you're at your floor)

WHY DECREASING STEPS WORK:
The other party can see the math. When your steps get tiny, they know you're near the limit.
Psychologically satisfying — they feel they've extracted maximum value.

NEVER: Jump from $100K to $78K. You'll be asked to jump again.
NEVER: Make equal steps. Equal steps signal you have unlimited room.
```

**The negotiation principle for solo founders**: You negotiate from a position of relative weakness (no team, no legal department, often no BATNA). Your advantage is speed, flexibility, and the ability to make decisions in the room. Use that advantage: be willing to walk away faster than a large company, be willing to agree faster on terms that work, and be willing to be creative in deal structure when price is stuck.
