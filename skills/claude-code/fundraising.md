## THE RAISE GATE (Mandatory — Fires Before Any Fundraising Advice)

Five questions. If you can't answer all five, you're not ready to raise.

```
RAISE GATE:
1. What's your current ARR? (VCs fund ARR, not MRR potential)
2. What is your YoY growth rate? (The number they care most about)
3. What's your net revenue retention? (Tells them if the business gets better or worse at scale)
4. Can you describe your ICP in 10 words and name 3 customers who'd give a reference call?
5. What do you need the money for — specifically? (Not "growth" — what will $1M of capital produce?)

Fail any → "You need 60-90 more days of data before fundraising conversations are productive."
Pass all → Proceed.
```

**The Paul Graham Rule**: "Investors are not your customers. They're funding your customers' access to you. Raise only when you know exactly what more capital produces — in specific revenue, users, or market position — that you cannot get without it."

## RAISE vs. STAY BOOTSTRAPPED DECISION FRAMEWORK

This is the most consequential decision a solo founder makes. Apply System 2 (deliberate analysis).

### The Bootstrapped Ceiling Analysis

First, calculate your "bootstrapped ceiling" — the maximum scale you can reach without external capital.

```
BOOTSTRAPPED CEILING:
Current ARR: $X
Current gross margin: Y%
Net profit margin (what you can reinvest): Z%
Monthly investable profit at current ARR: $X × Z% ÷ 12

At current growth rate, ARR in 36 months: $X × (1 + monthly_growth_rate)^36
Can you reach $5M ARR on retained earnings alone? → Yes/No

If yes → Seriously consider staying bootstrapped.
If no → Venture capital may be the only path to your scale target.
```

### The Raise Decision Matrix

| Scenario | Raise? | Why |
|---|---|---|
| <$100K ARR, no product-market fit | 🔴 No | VCs will pass. Use the time to find PMF. |
| $100K-500K ARR, 100%+ NRR, 3x YoY growth | 🟡 Seed if you need it | Could raise, but bootstrapping may be better |
| $500K-$2M ARR, 120%+ NRR, 2-3x YoY | ✅ Strong seed/pre-Series A | You have metrics. Raise if capital unlocks something specific. |
| $2M-$5M ARR, 120%+ NRR, 2x YoY | ✅ Series A ready | Most Series A funds will take the meeting |
| Any stage, strong bootstrapped cash flow | 🟡 Optional | Model both paths. Dilution costs are real. |
| Any stage, declining growth, <100% NRR | 🔴 Fix retention first | Capital accelerates the wrong thing |

**The Dilution Reality Check** (always run this before deciding):
```
Raising $2M at $10M valuation (20% dilution):

At exit scenario $50M: Your stake at 80% ownership = $40M
At exit scenario $50M: Your stake at 80% × 80% × 80% (3 rounds) = $32M
Without raising: 100% × $50M = $50M (if you can get there)

Dilution costs $18M at this scenario. Is the capital producing more than $18M in incremental value?
```

**The Sahil Lavingia (Gumroad) Principle**: "VC money is not free. It comes with expectations about growth rate and exit size. A $500K/year bootstrapped business might be better than a $5M/year VC-backed business where you own 8% and need a $200M exit to make money."

## STAGE-SPECIFIC FUNDRAISING PROTOCOLS

### Pre-Seed / Seed ($100K-$1M ARR)

**What investors are buying at seed**: The team's ability to figure things out. Not a proven model.
**What they actually look for**: Evidence of exceptional learning velocity, a real pain they understand deeply, and a plausible path to $1B+ market.

**Seed-stage metrics that matter**:
- MoM growth rate (>15% MoM for 6+ months = strong signal)
- NRR (>100% = customers are expanding, <100% = churn issue)
- Founder-market fit (why is THIS founder the right person for THIS problem?)
- Organic traction (how did first customers find you without paid ads?)

**What to prepare for seed**:
1. One-pager / deck: 10 slides max (problem, solution, traction, market, business model, team, ask)
2. Data room: MRR chart (at least 12 months), cohort retention, top-10 customer list
3. References: 3 customers who will take a 15-min call and say "I'd be devastated if this disappeared"
4. The ask: exactly how much, what it will produce specifically, what you'll accomplish in 18-24 months

**The YC Application Framework (use even if not applying)**:
- "What are you building, in 2 sentences?"
- "What problem does it solve, for whom, and how do you know?"
- "What's the most impressive thing about your traction?"
- "Why will you win against [competitor X]?"

**Seed check sizes and what to expect**:
- Pre-seed (angels/pre-product): $100K-$500K on a SAFE with $5M-10M cap
- Seed (angels + micro-VCs): $500K-$2M on a SAFE or priced round, $8M-15M valuation
- YC-backed seed: $500K from YC + $1-5M from demo day, ~7% dilution

### Series A ($1M-$5M ARR)

**What Series A investors are buying**: Evidence that the business model is repeatable and scalable.
**The Sean Ellis test for fundraising**: "Would your customers be very disappointed if you disappeared?" If <40%, fix retention before raising.

**Series A metrics (Christoph Janz / Point Nine benchmarks)**:
| Metric | Minimum to Get Meeting | Strong |
|---|---|---|
| ARR | $1M | $2M+ |
| YoY growth | 2x | 3x+ |
| Net Revenue Retention | 100% | 120%+ |
| Gross margin | 65%+ | 80%+ |
| Payback period | <18 months | <12 months |
| CAC:LTV | 1:3 | 1:5+ |

**What Series A investors will ask (prepare answers)**:
1. "Walk me through how your first 20 customers found you."
2. "What's your NRR, and how has it trended?"
3. "Describe your sales motion — is it repeatable without you?"
4. "If I gave you $5M today, how would you spend it and what would it produce in 18 months?"
5. "Who are your best competitors and why will you win?"
6. "What's your path to $50M ARR from here?"

**The Series A data room checklist**:
- [ ] MRR/ARR chart (24+ months)
- [ ] Cohort retention table (by signup month, 6+ months of data)
- [ ] Customer list (top 25 by ACV) with NPS/health score
- [ ] CAC and LTV by channel
- [ ] Gross margin calculation (with COGS breakdown)
- [ ] 3-year financial model (bottoms-up, not top-down)
- [ ] Org chart (current team + planned hires)
- [ ] Cap table (current and post-raise)
- [ ] Board/advisor list with bios
- [ ] Competitor landscape slide

## INVESTOR TYPES AND FIT MATRIX

### Type 1: Angels ($10K-$250K per check)

**Best for**: Pre-product, $0-$200K ARR, need validation + money + connections
**What they want**: Big market, exceptional founder, personal relevance to the problem
**How to find them**: LinkedIn → "investor" + your industry. AngelList. YC alumni network. Personal warm intros.
**What to give them**: 15-20% of a SAFE note with $5M-8M cap, no board seat

**The warm intro approach**:
"I'm raising $500K on a $7M SAFE cap. I'm looking for 3-5 angels who've built in [space]. [Mutual contact] thought you'd be a great fit. Here's the 2-pager. 15 minutes to explain the traction?"

### Type 2: Micro-VCs and Pre-Seed Funds ($100K-$1M per check)

**Best for**: $100K-$500K ARR, strong growth rate but not yet Series A ready
**Notable funds**: First Round (by referral), Precursor Ventures, Hustle Fund, Earnest Capital, Calm Company Fund (bootstrapped-first)
**What they want**: Repeatable unit economics, 10x+ return potential in 5-7 years
**How to find them**: OpenVC.app, airtable-based VC databases, AngelList Syndicates

### Type 3: Series A Funds ($1M-$8M per check)

**Notable SaaS-focused**: Bessemer, Redpoint, Battery, Menlo, Insight, OpenView (product-led growth specific)
**What they want**: $1M+ ARR, 2-3x YoY growth, clear path to $100M+ ARR
**Process**: 6-12 weeks from first meeting to term sheet. 2-4 partners involved.
**What to avoid**: Funds with no portfolio companies in your category (no thesis = no conviction = slow pass)

### Type 4: Bootstrapped-Friendly Capital (NEVER dilute if you don't have to)

**Earnest Capital / Calm Company Fund**:
- Royalty-based or SEAL structure (revenue sharing, not equity)
- For founders building profitable businesses, not "grow at all costs" VC-backed companies
- Check sizes: $50K-$1M
- Interest rate: 10-30% royalty until 3-5x cap returned
- Ideal for: $0-$2M ARR, high-margin business, bootstrapper mentality

**Revenue-based financing (Clearco, Capchase, Pipe)**:
- Advance 25-50% of MRR forward in exchange for revenue share
- Not equity. You keep ownership.
- Best for: $500K-$5M ARR, predictable SaaS revenue, need capital for growth (marketing, hiring)
- Cost: Effective APR 15-35% — compare against dilution cost before taking

## THE FINANCIAL NARRATIVE FRAMEWORK

Investors are not buying your current metrics. They're buying your story about where those metrics lead.

**The three-slide financial narrative**:

**Slide 1: Where we are**
"We're at $X ARR, growing Y% YoY with Z% NRR. Our unit economics are: LTV $X, CAC $Y, payback N months. We're [default alive / default dead]."

**Slide 2: The model that scales**
"The reason this compounds: [mechanism]. As we grow from $X to $50M ARR, [the economics improve/stay stable/degrade but are managed by X] because [specific structural reason]. Here's the bottoms-up model."

**Slide 3: What the capital produces**
"$[Amount] raised enables: [specific hires + specific outcomes + specific MRR target in 18 months]. At $[target MRR], we'd raise a Series [X] at $[target valuation]. Here's how the math works."

**The bottoms-up model (investors will stress-test this)**:
```
Month 1-6: 3 enterprise AEs hired ($120K OTE each)
Each AE closes 2 deals/month at $3K ACV (historical close rate: 25% of demos)
New MRR from sales: 3 × 2 × $250/mo = $1,500/mo additional per month

Product hire: 1 senior engineer ($180K)
Shipping velocity: current 2 features/sprint → 4 features/sprint
Feature velocity → retention improvement: 2% reduction in monthly churn (model shows +$12K ARR/mo from this)

Total: $1M investment → $X MRR at 18 months
IRR for investor at projected Series A valuation: Y%
```

## TERM SHEET BASICS

### SAFE Notes (Simple Agreement for Future Equity)

The standard seed instrument. Know these terms:

**Valuation cap**: The maximum valuation at which your SAFE converts to equity.
- Lower cap = more founder-friendly to investor
- Negotiate: "What's the highest cap you'd do?" Start there.

**Discount**: Typically 15-20%. Means SAFE investors convert at 80-85% of Series A price.
- More important when you don't have a cap
- If you have both cap and discount: investor gets the better of the two — favor cap-only SAFEs

**Pro-rata rights**: The right to invest in the next round to maintain their percentage.
- Standard to give. Not giving it signals you don't want them involved long-term.

**MFN (Most Favored Nation) clause**: If you give a better deal to a future SAFE investor, existing investors automatically get the same.
- Standard and fine.

### Priced Round Terms to Understand

**Pre-money vs post-money valuation**: You raise $2M at $10M pre-money → post-money = $12M. Investor owns 2/12 = 16.7%. You own less.

**Participating preferred vs. non-participating**:
- Non-participating preferred: investors get their money back OR convert to common. Standard. Founder-friendly.
- Participating preferred: investors get their money back AND convert to common ("double dip"). Avoid this.

**Liquidation preference**: 1x non-participating is standard. 2x or 3x means investor gets 2-3x their money before you see anything in most exit scenarios.

**Anti-dilution (broad-based weighted average)**: Standard. Protects investors if you raise a down round at a lower valuation. Fair.

**Full ratchet anti-dilution**: If you raise at a lower valuation later, investors' shares multiply to maintain their percentage. EXTREMELY founder-hostile. Do not accept.

**Board composition**: For Series A, expect investor to request one board seat. Standard. Two investor seats = they can control the board. Avoid until Series B+.

### Term Sheet Red Flags

| Term | Red Flag Level | Why |
|---|---|---|
| 3x or higher liquidation preference | 🔴 Critical | You will likely never see money in an acquisition |
| Full ratchet anti-dilution | 🔴 Critical | Can obliterate founder ownership in a down round |
| Investor approval for any expenditure >$X | 🔴 Critical | Removes your operating autonomy |
| 2+ board seats for first institutional round | 🟡 Warning | They can outvote you on key decisions |
| Participating preferred at Series A | 🟡 Warning | Reduces your economics in acquisitions significantly |
| Broad redemption rights | 🟡 Warning | They can force a sale you don't want |
| Right of first refusal on secondary sales | 🟢 Standard | Normal and fine |

**The lawyer rule**: Do NOT sign a term sheet without a startup-specialized attorney reviewing it. A qualified startup lawyer costs $2-5K for term sheet review. A bad term can cost you millions. This is the one place to spend on legal without question.

## THE FUNDRAISING PROCESS (Timeline and Mechanics)

### 60-Day Fundraising Sprint

Investors make decisions in batches. Create competitive urgency or you'll be perpetually "in diligence."

```
WEEK 1-2: Preparation
- [ ] Finalize deck (10 slides, 15-second pitch version and 3-minute version)
- [ ] Build target investor list (50+ for seed, 20+ for Series A)
- [ ] Prioritize: A-tier (dream), B-tier (realistic), C-tier (backup)
- [ ] Prepare data room (secure folder access only on request)
- [ ] Brief your customer references — they'll get calls

WEEK 3-4: First meetings (B-tier first)
- [ ] Practice pitch on friendly/B-tier investors first
- [ ] Take notes on every objection — iterate the deck
- [ ] Do NOT share valuation cap until late in conversation (let them ask)

WEEK 5-6: A-tier meetings
- [ ] Move to A-tier with improved pitch
- [ ] "We have several meetings this week and next, planning to close in 30 days"
  (Creates urgency — only say this if true)

WEEK 7-8: Close
- [ ] First term sheet → call all other investors immediately: "We have a term sheet. Can you move to a decision this week?"
- [ ] Sign and close

NEVER: Keep raising indefinitely with no close date. Investors wait each other out. Set and hold a close date.
```

### The Pitch Meeting Structure

**First 90 seconds**: The hook. What you do, for whom, and why it's working.
"We've built [product] for [specific ICP]. In [X months], we've grown to $[ARR] with [NRR]% NRR. Here's how it compounds."

**Minutes 2-10**: Traction walk. Don't tell — show. Month-by-month MRR chart. Cohort retention chart. One or two customer stories.

**Minutes 10-20**: The ask and the model. Where this goes with capital, specifically.

**Minutes 20-30**: Questions. Listen more than you talk.

**End**: "What would I need to show you to get to a yes?" Ask directly. It saves 4 weeks of ambiguity.

## POST-FUNDRAISE OPERATIONAL CHANGES

Many founders don't realize how much changes after raising. Know before you decide.

### What raising $500K-$2M from institutional investors means:

1. **Board meeting cadence**: Monthly or quarterly board updates expected. Prepare a board deck. Budget 1-2 days/month for investor relations.

2. **Legal compliance**: Annual report, 83(b) elections for any new option grants, 409A valuation required every 12 months ($5K-15K/year).

3. **Accounting standards**: Move from cash-basis (fine for bootstrapped) to accrual-basis (required for VC-backed). Hire a startup CPA if you haven't.

4. **Employee option grants**: Options pool (ESOP) typically 10-15% of cap table post-close. Every new hire needs a grant agreement and vesting schedule.

5. **Expectation alignment**: Seed investors expect you to raise a Series A in 18-24 months. If growth doesn't support it, difficult conversations follow.

6. **The "founder-friendly" test**: Before signing: talk to 3 founders the investor backed who did NOT have a successful exit. How did the investor behave when things got hard?

## THE BOOTSTRAPPED ALTERNATIVE (Run This Before Deciding to Raise)

For every fundraising conversation, model the bootstrapped path first.

```
BOOTSTRAPPED PATH MODEL:
Current ARR: $X
Current profit margin: Y% (after all expenses including your salary)
Monthly reinvestable profit: $X × Y% ÷ 12

Question 1: Can you hire one person at current profit rate?
  → At Y% margin on $X ARR, investable = $Z/month
  → $Z/month × 12 = $Z/year hiring budget

Question 2: At current growth rate, what's ARR in 24 months?
  → $X × (1 + monthly_growth_rate)^24 = $Y
  → Is $Y a meaningful business? (Y/N)

Question 3: What does $1M in VC produce that $1M from customers doesn't?
  → If the answer is "speed" → quantify the speed: $1M in VC gets us to $5M ARR 12 months faster than organic
  → Is 12 months worth 20-25% dilution? (Model the math)

If you can reach your target without raising → consider very carefully whether to raise.
Many $1M-$10M ARR businesses are worth more to their founder as bootstrapped cash-flowing assets
than as VC-backed businesses with 3 rounds of dilution.
```

**The Gumroad lesson**: Sahil Lavingia raised $8M from Kleiner Perkins, grew to 9 employees, then had to lay off 75% of the team when VC growth expectations weren't met. He returned to solo-founder model, grew to $20M+ GMV, and is more satisfied. The business was always profitable — the VC capital created pressure that nearly destroyed it.

## KILL SIGNALS

```
KILL SIGNAL — Should you keep fundraising?
If after 30 investor meetings you have zero term sheets → the story isn't working OR the metrics aren't there.
Stop raising. Fix the underlying issue. Come back with better numbers.
Do not spend a full year raising at the wrong stage.

KILL SIGNAL — Is this the right investor?
If you can't get 3 references from their portfolio companies who'd work with them again → pass.
Investor reference calls are as important as investor due diligence on you.

KILL SIGNAL — Is the raise working?
If at 90 days post-close you cannot clearly state what the capital is producing (in MRR, in users, in hires that shipped) → review the capital allocation. Capital going nowhere is the most common post-raise mistake.
```

## THE ARTHASHASTRA ON TREASURY MANAGEMENT

Kautilya, Book 2, Chapter 1: "The treasury is the root of the kingdom. Without a full treasury, the state cannot function."

Applied to startups: **Cash is not a growth problem. Cash is an existence problem.** The fundraising decision must be evaluated against the "default alive" question first (see finance.md). Raising capital to survive is different from raising capital to accelerate.

Kautilya's principle of **Artha** (prosperity through action): Capital raised is neutral. It takes the character of how it is deployed. Capital deployed into validated growth channels compounds. Capital deployed into unvalidated bets evaporates. The discipline of the founder determines whether the raise was worth it.

Sun Tzu on capital: "Victorious warriors win first and then go to war, while defeated warriors go to war first and then seek to win." Raise when you have leverage (strong metrics). Not when you need it (weak position). Metrics are your leverage. Raise from strength, not desperation.
