# POSITIONING — Narrative Architecture Engine

## Auto-Trigger (No Slash Command Needed)

Fires automatically when founder says:
- "how do I explain what we do"
- "nobody gets it when I describe the product"
- "my messaging isn't landing"
- "what's our positioning"
- "elevator pitch" / "one-liner" / "tagline"
- "how should I describe this to [ICP]"
- "we're competing with X but we're different"
- "value proposition" / "category creation"
- "I keep having to explain why we're not X"
- "our landing page isn't converting"

**Positioning fires BEFORE launch and BEFORE any content/SEO/sales work.** Weak positioning is not a messaging problem — it's a strategy problem that makes every downstream activity less effective.

---

## WHY THIS EXISTS

April Dunford's research (Obviously Awesome, 2019) analyzed 200+ B2B startups and found:

> "The vast majority of positioning failures are not messaging failures. They are failures to understand the competitive alternative customers actually compare you to."

The #1 positioning error: founders position their product against a competitor when buyers are actually comparing it against doing nothing, using a spreadsheet, or building it themselves.

The #2 positioning error: leading with features before establishing WHY those features matter to this specific customer.

**Correct positioning order**: Competitive alternative → What makes you different → Why that matters → Who it matters most to → What category you live in. Reverse this and you have a features brochure, not a position.

## THE DUNFORD POSITIONING PROTOCOL

Run this 5-step process to produce a complete positioning foundation.

### Step 1: Identify the TRUE Competitive Alternative

The question is not "who are your competitors?" — it's "what would a customer do if your product didn't exist?"

```
COMPETITIVE ALTERNATIVE AUDIT:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Ask your last 10 customers: "What were you doing before you found us?"

Typical answers fall into 5 categories:
□ Spreadsheets / manual process (most common for process automation tools)
□ A specific named competitor product
□ Hiring someone to do it manually (services as alternative)
□ DIY / building it themselves (engineering teams)
□ Doing nothing / living with the problem

The CORRECT competitive set is whatever customers say — not what you assume.

CRITICAL INSIGHT: If customers say "spreadsheets" and you're positioning against Salesforce:
  - Your pricing is wrong (compared to free, not $150/user)
  - Your marketing is wrong (going to the wrong places)
  - Your differentiation is wrong (wrong features emphasized)
  - You're solving for the wrong objections in sales

POSITIONING ALTERNATIVE MATRIX:
| Customer said | Real alternative | Implication |
|---|---|---|
| Spreadsheets | $0, manual, familiar | Emphasize time savings + error reduction |
| [Named competitor] | Specific features | Emphasize what you have that they don't |
| Manual process / person | Hiring cost | Emphasize ROI vs. salary |
| Nothing / lived with it | Free status quo | Emphasize activation cost (urgency) |
| Built it themselves | Engineering time | Emphasize build vs. buy math |
```

**Output Step 1**:
```
PRIMARY COMPETITIVE ALTERNATIVE: [What customers are actually comparing you to]
SECONDARY ALTERNATIVE: [Second most common]
IMPLICATIONS: [How this changes your pricing/marketing/sales approach]
```

### Step 2: Identify Your Unique Attributes

What does your product have or do that the competitive alternative does NOT?

```
UNIQUE ATTRIBUTE DISCOVERY:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Three categories of attributes:

CATEGORY A: UNIQUE AND PROVABLE
Features, capabilities, or properties that:
  - You actually have
  - The primary alternative doesn't have
  - You can demonstrate in a demo or proof
These are your positioning anchors. List all of them.

CATEGORY B: BETTER BUT NOT UNIQUE
Things both you and alternatives do, but you do better.
These are NOT positioning differentiators — they're table stakes claims.
Saying "we're faster" when alternatives are also fast → nobody believes it.

CATEGORY C: UNIQUE BUT IRRELEVANT
Things only you have but that don't translate to customer value.
These are engineering pride items, not positioning items.

EXERCISE: List every feature/attribute of your product.
For each: Can the primary alternative do this? → Yes = B or C, No = A candidate
For each A candidate: Does a customer care? → No = Category C, Yes = ANCHOR
```

**Output Step 2**:
```
UNIQUE ATTRIBUTES (Category A only):
1. [Attribute] — [why alternative can't match this]
2. [Attribute] — [why alternative can't match this]
3. [Attribute] — [why alternative can't match this]
```

---

### Step 3: Map Attributes to Value (The "So What?" Test)

For each unique attribute, ask: "so what does that actually mean for the customer?"

```
THE "SO WHAT?" CHAIN:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Attribute → Capability → Benefit → Value → Business outcome

EXAMPLE:
Attribute: "Real-time sync across all devices"
  → Capability: Your data is always current wherever you access it
  → Benefit: No version confusion, no manually merging updates
  → Value: Less time reconciling, fewer errors, faster decisions
  → Business outcome: X hours/week saved per user, Y errors/month eliminated

THE POSITIONING RULE: Customers buy at the "Business outcome" level,
                      not at the "Attribute" level.
Lead with the outcome. Prove it with the attribute.

BAD: "Our software has real-time sync"
GOOD: "Stop losing [X hours] per week reconciling spreadsheet versions"
```

**Output Step 3**:
```
ATTRIBUTE → VALUE MAP:
1. [Attribute] → [Business outcome for ICP]
2. [Attribute] → [Business outcome for ICP]
3. [Attribute] → [Business outcome for ICP]

HEADLINE VALUE (the biggest business outcome): [One sentence]
```

---

### Step 4: Identify the Best-Fit Customer Segment

Who values your unique attributes MOST?

```
SEGMENT VALUATION MATRIX:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Not all customers care equally about your attributes.
The best-fit customer is the one who:
  (a) Has the problem your unique attribute solves most acutely
  (b) Can actually feel the value in their specific workflow
  (c) Has budget to pay what you need to charge
  (d) Is reachable through channels you can access

SEGMENT SCORING (rate each prospect type 1-10 on each dimension):
| Segment | Problem acuity | Value felt | Budget fit | Reachability | TOTAL |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|

Highest total = primary positioning target.

COMMON MISTAKE: Positioning to the largest segment, not the most acute segment.
                The most acute segment is smallest but converts fastest, pays most,
                and becomes your loudest advocates. Start there.
```

**Output Step 4**:
```
PRIMARY SEGMENT (best fit for unique attributes):
  Job title: [exact role]
  Company type: [size, industry, stage]
  Their acute version of the problem: [specific description]
  Why YOUR attributes matter most to them: [specific reason]

POSITIONING STATEMENT (internal use):
"For [primary segment] who [have this problem],
[Product] is the [category] that [primary value/outcome]
unlike [competitive alternative] which [limitation]."
```

### Step 5: Select the Market Category Frame

The category frame is the mental shelf customers put your product on. Wrong frame = wrong comparison set = wrong expectations = lower conversion.

```
CATEGORY FRAME OPTIONS:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

OPTION A: HEAD-TO-HEAD IN EXISTING CATEGORY
  Use when: A well-known category exists and you're clearly better for your segment
  Risk: You inherit the baggage of the category (assumptions, objections, price expectations)
  Reward: Zero education cost — customers immediately understand what you do
  Signal to use: Customers already use or have heard of products in this space

OPTION B: SUBCATEGORY CREATION
  Use when: You're better for a SPECIFIC type of customer within an existing category
  Example: "CRM for e-commerce" (not just CRM) / "Analytics for non-technical teams" (not just Analytics)
  Reward: Inherit category awareness + differentiate from generic players
  Signal to use: Customers say "we need a [existing category] but one that does [specific thing]"

OPTION C: NEW CATEGORY CREATION
  Use when: Your solution is genuinely different enough that no existing frame fits
  Risk: Maximum education cost. Investors and customers both need education.
  Reward: Own the category if you win. No direct competitors.
  Signal to use: Customers say "I've never seen anything like this"
  WARNING: Most founders think they need a new category when they don't.
           Only create a new category when subcategory creation genuinely fails.

CATEGORY SELECTION DECISION TREE:
1. Is there an existing category that customers already understand? → Yes: go to A or B
2. Does your product serve a specific segment of that category better? → Yes: B (subcategory)
3. Is your product genuinely different enough that existing categories misrepresent it? → Yes: C
4. Is the difference customer-felt or just founder-believed? → Founder-believed only: stay in existing category
```

**Output Step 5**:
```
MARKET CATEGORY: [Name of category or subcategory]
CATEGORY RATIONALE: [Why this frame helps, not hurts]
CATEGORY RISKS: [What baggage or wrong assumptions this frame brings]
```

## POSITIONING STATEMENT GENERATOR

After completing the 5 steps, produce these deliverables:

### A. The 25-Word Positioning Statement (External)

```
FORMULA:
[Product] helps [exact ICP] [do/achieve specific outcome] without [the thing they hate about alternatives].

EXAMPLES:
"Loom helps remote teams share context instantly without scheduling a meeting."
"Superhuman helps executives reach inbox zero twice as fast without learning new email habits."
"Notion helps early-stage startups replace 5 tools with one without losing the flexibility they need."

RULES:
- No jargon. If your ICP wouldn't say this word, remove it.
- The "without" clause is mandatory — it names your real competitive differentiation.
- 25 words or fewer. If you need more: you're not positioned yet.
```

### B. The 3 Differentiation Proof Points

```
FORMAT: [Claim] — [Proof]

Each proof point must be:
- Tied to a unique attribute (from Step 2)
- Expressed as a customer outcome (from Step 3)
- Provable (number, case study, or demo)

EXAMPLE:
1. "3x faster onboarding than [alternative]" — customers go from signup to first value in <5 minutes vs. 15+ minutes
2. "No IT involvement required" — 94% of customers deploy without contacting IT once
3. "Built for [specific ICP] workflows" — [specific feature] that [alternative] doesn't support
```

### C. The Objection Map

```
OBJECTION MAPPING (positioning for sales conversations):
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Objection 1: "We already use [primary alternative]"
RESPONSE FRAMEWORK:
- Acknowledge: "A lot of our customers came from [alternative]"
- Bridge: "What made them switch was [unique attribute] — [alternative] doesn't do [specific thing]"
- Proof: "[Customer name] moved from [alternative] and [specific outcome they got]"

Objection 2: "Your price is higher than [alternative]"
RESPONSE FRAMEWORK:
- Reframe alternative: "Compare us to [what they'd replace] — [alternative] is $X/month, we're $Y"
- ROI bridge: "Our customers average [outcome] — at [frequency], that's [$ value] vs. our $Y price"
- Ask: "If you could quantify the value of [primary outcome] — what would that be worth to you?"

Objection 3: "We're not ready / too early / good enough for now"
RESPONSE FRAMEWORK:
- Quantify the "good enough" cost: "Manual [process] costs [X hours × person's hourly rate]"
- Timeline anchor: "What would need to be true for this to move up your priority list?"
- Reduce friction: "We have [trial / implementation support / migration help] — what's the first thing to prove?"
```

## POSITIONING AUDIT (For Existing Products)

Run when: landing page isn't converting, sales conversations are confusing, or messaging feels off.

```
POSITIONING AUDIT CHECKLIST:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

□ ALTERNATIVE TEST: Can you name the #1 thing customers do without your product?
  → If no: you haven't done customer interviews. Stop. Do 10 interviews first.

□ DIFFERENTIATION TEST: Is your "unique" feature actually unique?
  → Check: Does [primary alternative] do this? If yes: not differentiating.

□ ICP PRECISION TEST: Who is your positioning written for?
  → If "small businesses" or "startups" → too broad. Name the job title, not the category.

□ PROOF TEST: Can you prove every claim in your positioning?
  → If you say "saves time" → what's the exact time saved (measured from which cohort)?

□ CATEGORY FRAME TEST: What mental shelf do customers put you on?
  → Ask 5 prospects: "How would you describe what we do to a colleague?"
  → If answers differ wildly: no shared frame. Positioning is failing.

□ LANDING PAGE ABOVE-THE-FOLD TEST:
  → Spend 5 seconds on your landing page. Answer: what does this product do?
    - For WHO?
    - Instead of WHAT?
    - Creating WHAT outcome?
  → If any of these are unclear in 5 seconds: above-the-fold positioning is broken.

□ THE STRANGER TEST:
  → Read your one-liner to someone who doesn't know your product.
    "What do you think this company does?"
    If their answer is wrong: your positioning is wrong, not them.
```

## COMPETITIVE POSITIONING MAP (Positioning Against Alternatives)

```
POSITIONING MAP BUILDER:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Step 1: Select the 2 dimensions that matter most to your ICP.
(These are the axes where you win — do NOT pick random axes)

Example dimensions (pick 2 that apply to your market):
- Ease of use ↔ Power/configurability
- Speed of value ↔ Depth of features
- Price ↔ Capability
- Self-serve ↔ White-glove service
- Built for [specific ICP] ↔ General purpose
- Vertical-specific ↔ Horizontal

Step 2: Plot all competitive alternatives on these 2 axes.

Step 3: Find the quadrant where you WIN and alternatives are absent or weak.
That quadrant = your positioning territory. Own it. Name it. Defend it.

RULE: Your positioning should describe why you WIN in your quadrant,
      not why you're competitive in ALL quadrants.
      "Best for [specific use case] when [specific condition]" outperforms
      "The best [category] for everyone."
```

## CATEGORY CREATION FRAMEWORK (For Future Vision Archetype)

Only proceed here if Step 5 resulted in "New Category Creation."

```
CATEGORY CREATION REQUIREMENTS (all 5 must be true):
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

□ Your solution is genuinely novel — no existing frame captures it
□ You have documented customer evidence that existing frames cause confusion
□ You have a clear name for the new category (noun + modifier)
□ You have a "creation myth" — a compelling explanation of WHY this category needs to exist NOW
□ You have the resources to educate the market (this takes 12-36 months and $$$)

CATEGORY NAME FORMULA: [Adjective] + [Noun] where noun is recognizable and adjective is differentiating
Examples: "Conversational Commerce" / "Revenue Intelligence" / "Continuous Documentation"

CATEGORY CREATION ASSETS (must produce all before claiming the category):
1. Category manifesto (1,000-2,000 word essay explaining the problem + why now)
2. Industry data point that validates the category need
3. 3 case studies showing transformation (before = status quo pain, after = new way)
4. A named framework or methodology that owns the category intellectually
5. A community or event associated with the category (not just the product)

WARNING: Category creation without significant marketing resources and >$1M ARR is almost always
         premature. Most founders who believe they need category creation actually need better
         subcategory positioning. The test: can you name 5 customers who'd say "we're in this category"
         without prompting? If no: category doesn't exist yet.
```

## INTEGRATION

| Skill | Relationship |
|---|---|
| `validate.md` | Positioning must be tested with real buyers during validation — not invented in a vacuum |
| `pmf.md` | PMF and positioning are linked — "Very Disappointed" users' words are your positioning copy |
| `launch.md` | Every launch asset uses the positioning statement — launch before positioning = wasted effort |
| `sales.md` | Objection map from positioning becomes the sales conversation framework |
| `content.md` | Positioning determines what content topics own your category |
| `brand.md` | Brand voice follows from positioning — who you are is downstream of who you serve |
| `decide.md` | "Should we reposition?" is a major decision — run through decide.md with 2/10 reversibility |

**The positioning rule for solo founders**: You cannot do positioning once and be done. It evolves as you learn more about who your best customers are and what they actually value. Revisit every 90 days until you have clear PMF. After PMF: revisit every 6 months or after any major product change.
