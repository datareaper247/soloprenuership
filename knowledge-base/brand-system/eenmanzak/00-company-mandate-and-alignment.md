# Company Mandate and Documentation Alignment

**Status:** Draft strategic alignment; founder review required
**Last updated:** 2026-04-18
**Purpose:** Align the entire brand-system pack with the broader company goal: an AI-first founder-led company using senior software engineering expertise in ecommerce and logistics to build B2B/B2C apps, SaaS/micro-SaaS, consulting, R&D, OSS, content authority, and parallel revenue streams.

> This document supersedes any earlier reading that made Dutch ZZP tooling, SoloOS, TapTap, SpatialSense, or soloprenuership the entire company. Those are lanes, assets, or experiments. The company mandate is broader.

## 1. Updated company mandate

Build an **AI-first founder-led software company** from the Netherlands that turns senior engineering expertise into multiple compounding revenue and trust assets:

- B2C apps;
- B2B apps;
- SaaS and micro-SaaS;
- ecommerce and logistics workflow products;
- consulting and productized services;
- R&D-backed product bets;
- OSS and public engineering artifacts;
- high-authority content and personal branding;
- social trust, visibility, and distribution;
- internal AI operating systems that increase speed, quality, and leverage.

Long-term ambition: grow from a solo-founder operating system into a durable multinational company, while preserving quality, trust, compliance, and personal risk controls.

## 2. Founder advantage

The founder positioning should emphasize:

- senior software engineering judgment;
- ecommerce and logistics domain expertise;
- ability to build production systems, not just prototypes;
- practical AI-first execution;
- ability to translate operational pain into products;
- credibility across B2B, B2C, and internal tooling;
- public learning and proof through launches, OSS, demos, and writing.

## 4. Documentation interpretation rule

When any existing document says “primary wedge,” “company thesis,” “solo-founder product studio,” “Dutch ZZP,” or “SoloOS,” interpret it through this hierarchy:

1. company mission: AI-first software company using founder engineering/domain expertise;
2. domain authority: ecommerce, logistics, B2B/B2C software, operations, product execution;
3. revenue streams: apps, SaaS/micro-SaaS, consulting, R&D, OSS/content-led distribution;
4. individual project lanes: Dutch ZZP, TapTap, SpatialSense, SoloOS, other products.

No individual project lane owns the company narrative until it proves demand through 2–3 launch cycles or material revenue/proof.

## 5. Strategic guardrails

- Do not over-narrow the company to Dutch ZZP unless that lane proves strongest.
- Do not over-narrow the company to SoloOS until it proves repeatable external demand.
- Do not over-narrow the company to consulting; use services as cashflow and discovery.
- Do not over-narrow the company to apps; app-store products are one revenue channel.
- Do not create a portfolio that looks scattered; each lane must connect to founder expertise, proof, or revenue.
- Do not claim MNC-scale maturity before it exists; frame it as long-term ambition backed by operating systems and launch cycles.
- Keep compliance, liability, and personal-wealth protection as design constraints, not afterthoughts.

## 6. Recommended public umbrella positioning for review

### Option A — domain-led AI-first software company

> An AI-first software company building practical products for ecommerce, logistics, and solo/operator workflows — from apps and micro-SaaS to consulting and R&D-backed product bets.

### Option B — founder-led product company

> A founder-led software company turning senior engineering experience in ecommerce and logistics into useful apps, SaaS products, services, and public engineering proof.

### Option C — execution-led company builder

> Building a portfolio of high-quality B2B and B2C software products with AI-first execution, public proof, and deep operating-domain expertise.

## 7. Near-term execution focus

Until 2–3 launch cycles prove otherwise, run the company as a **portfolio learning engine**:

1. one trust spine;
2. one founder authority narrative;
3. one domain authority stream around ecommerce/logistics/software execution;
4. one service/cashflow offer;
5. one app/product launch lane;
6. one micro-SaaS validation lane;
7. one OSS/public engineering proof lane.

The first 2–3 launch cycles should decide what the company becomes more publicly.
